Interesting Facts about Meteorites

Origins: Meteorites are fragments of asteroids, comets, or other celestial bodies that have fallen to Earth. Most meteorites originate from the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter. (NASA Science)
Oldest Rocks: Meteorites are among the oldest objects in the solar system, with some dating back 4.6 billion years. These ancient rocks provide valuable information about the early solar system. (Space.com)
Types of Meteorites: There are three main types of meteorites: stony (chondrites and achondrites), iron, and stony-iron (pallasites and mesosiderites). Stony meteorites are the most common, making up about 94% of all known meteorites. (National History Museum)
Iron Meteorites: Iron meteorites, composed mostly of iron and nickel, come from the cores of differentiated asteroids that melted and separated into layers, similar to the layers within Earth. The Hoba meteorite in Namibia is the largest known meteorite, weighing about 60 tons. (The Planets)
Largest Meteorite Crater: The Vredefort Crater in South Africa is the largest confirmed impact crater on Earth, with an estimated original diameter of 300 kilometers (186 miles). (Wikipedia)
Meteor Showers: Meteor showers occur when Earth passes through the debris trail left by a comet. The Perseids and Leonids are well-known meteor showers resulting from Earth passing through the debris of comets Swift-Tuttle and Tempel-Tuttle, respectively. (National History Museum)
Martian Meteorites: Some meteorites found on Earth originated from Mars, ejected by impacts on the Martian surface. These meteorites contain clues about the Martian atmosphere and geology. (Space.com)
Impact Events: The Chelyabinsk meteorite that exploded over Russia in 2013 caused widespread damage and injuries due to its shockwave. It was the largest recorded meteorite event since the Tunguska event in 1908. (Wikipedia)
Building Blocks of Life: Some meteorites contain organic compounds, including amino acids, which are the building blocks of life. This has led to theories that meteorites may have played a role in the origin of life on Earth. (NASA Science)
Rare Human Impact: There have only been a couple of confirmed cases of humans being hit by a meteorite. The most famous case is that of Ann Hodges in 1954, who was struck by a meteorite in Alabama. (Smithsonian Institute)
